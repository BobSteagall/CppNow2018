<p><b>Modern Standard Hindi</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hindi_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindi language">Hindi</a>: <span lang="hi" xml:lang="hi">मानक हिन्दी <i>mānak hindī</i></span>) or simply <b>Hindi</b> (Hindi: <span lang="hi" xml:lang="hi"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%A4%B9%E0%A4%BF%E0%A4%A8%E0%A5%8D%E0%A4%A6%E0%A5%80" class="extiw" title="wikt:हिन्दी">हिन्दी</a> <i>hindī</i></span>), is a <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">standardised</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit#Influence_on_other_languages" title="Sanskrit">Sanskritised</a> <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">register</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Hindustani language</a>. Hindi is one of the official languages of the Union of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/Hindi_belt" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindi belt">Hindi belt languages</a>.</p>
<p>Hindi is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_languages_by_number_of_native_speakers" title="List of languages by number of native speakers">fourth-most spoken first language</a> in the world, after <a href="/wiki/Standard_Mandarin_Chinese" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard Mandarin Chinese">Standard Mandarin Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nationalencyklopedin_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nationalencyklopedin-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Official_status">Official status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Official status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Article 343 (1) of the Indian constitution states:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The official language of the Union shall be Hindi in <a href="/wiki/Devanagari" title="Devanagari">Devanagari</a> script. The form of numerals to be used for the official purposes of the Union shall be the international form of Indian numerals.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Article 351 of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian constitution">Indian constitution</a> states</p>
<blockquote>
<p>It shall be the duty of the Union to promote the spread of the Hindi language, to develop it so that it may serve as a medium of expression for all the elements of the composite culture of India and to secure its enrichment by assimilating without interfering with its genius, the forms, style and expressions used in Hindustani and in the other languages of India specified in the Eighth Schedule, and by drawing, wherever necessary or desirable, for its vocabulary, primarily on Sanskrit and secondarily on other languages.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>It was envisioned that Hindi would become the sole working language of the Union Government by 1965 (per directives in Article 344 (2) and Article 351),<sup id="cite_ref-eighthschedule_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eighthschedule-8">[8]</a></sup> with state governments being free to function in the language of their own choice. However, widespread resistance to the imposition of Hindi on non-native speakers, especially in <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">South India</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Hindi_agitations_of_Tamil_Nadu" title="Anti-Hindi agitations of Tamil Nadu">those in Tamil Nadu</a>), <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andhra_Pradesh" title="Andhra Pradesh">Andhra Pradesh</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Bengal" title="West Bengal">West Bengal</a>, led to the passage of the Official Languages Act of 1963, which provided for the continued use of English indefinitely for all official purposes, although the constitutional directive for the Union Government to encourage the spread of Hindi was retained and has strongly influenced its policies.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>At the state level, Hindi is the official language of the following Indian states: <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chhattisgarh" title="Chhattisgarh">Chhattisgarh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haryana" title="Haryana">Haryana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Himachal_Pradesh" title="Himachal Pradesh">Himachal Pradesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jharkhand" title="Jharkhand">Jharkhand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madhya_Pradesh" title="Madhya Pradesh">Madhya Pradesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Uttarakhand" title="Uttarakhand">Uttarakhand</a>. Each may also designate a "co-official language"; in Uttar Pradesh, for instance, depending on the political formation in power, this language is generally <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a>. Similarly, Hindi is accorded the status of official language in the following <a href="/wiki/Union_Territories" class="mw-redirect" title="Union Territories">Union Territories</a>: <a href="/wiki/Andaman_%26_Nicobar_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Andaman &amp; Nicobar Islands">Andaman &amp; Nicobar Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chandigarh" title="Chandigarh">Chandigarh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dadra_%26_Nagar_Haveli" class="mw-redirect" title="Dadra &amp; Nagar Haveli">Dadra &amp; Nagar Haveli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daman_%26_Diu" class="mw-redirect" title="Daman &amp; Diu">Daman &amp; Diu</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Capital_Territory" class="mw-redirect" title="National Capital Territory">National Capital Territory</a>.</p>
<p>National-language status for Hindi is a long-debated theme. An Indian court clarified that Hindi is not the national language of India because the constitution does not mention it as such.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_India">Outside India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Outside India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Outside <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> a form of Hindi is an official language in <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>. However, this is not the same language as the official language of India. The Hindi spoken in Fiji is <a href="/wiki/Fiji_Hindi" title="Fiji Hindi">Fiji Hindi</a>, a form of <a href="/wiki/Awadhi_language" title="Awadhi language">Awadhi</a>, whereas the Modern Standard Hindi of India is a form of <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Hindustani</a>. Hindi is also spoken by a large population of <a href="/wiki/Madheshi" class="mw-redirect" title="Madheshi">Madheshis</a> (people having roots in north-India but have migrated to Nepal over hundreds of years) of <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>. Hindi is quite easy to understand for some <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistanis</a>, who speak <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a>, which, like Hindi, is part of Hindustani. Apart from this, Hindi is spoken by the large <a href="/wiki/Indian_diaspora" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian diaspora">Indian diaspora</a> which hails from, or has its origin from the "Hindi Belt" of India. A substantially large Indian diaspora lives in countries like The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, The <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> and few other countries, where it is natively spoken at home and among their own communities. Outside <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, Hindi speakers are 100,000 in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>; 485,170 in <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>; 250,292 in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>; 232,760 in <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a>; 107,000 in <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>; 3,000 in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>; 8 million in <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>; 20,000 in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>; 20,000 in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Hindustani" title="History of Hindustani">History of Hindustani</a></div>
<p>Like other Indo-Aryan languages, Hindi is considered to be a direct descendant of an early form of <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>, through <a href="/wiki/Sauraseni_Prakrit" class="mw-redirect" title="Sauraseni Prakrit">Sauraseni Prakrit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apabhra%E1%B9%83%C5%9Ba" title="Apabhraṃśa">Śauraseni Apabhraṃśa</a>. It has been influenced by <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">Dravidian languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Hindi emerged as <a href="/wiki/Apabhramsha" class="mw-redirect" title="Apabhramsha">Apabhramsha</a> (Sanskrit:अपभ्रंश; Corruption or corrupted speech), a degenerated form of <a href="/wiki/Prakrit" title="Prakrit">Prakrit</a>, in the 7th century A.D. By the 10th century A.D., it became stable. <a href="/wiki/Braj" title="Braj">Braj</a>, <a href="/wiki/Awadhi" class="mw-redirect" title="Awadhi">Awadhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khari_Boli" class="mw-redirect" title="Khari Boli">Khari Boli</a> etc. are the dialects of Hindi. The dialect of <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Hindustani</a> on which Standard Hindi is based is <i><a href="/wiki/Khariboli_dialect" title="Khariboli dialect">Khariboli</a></i>, the vernacular of <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a> and the surrounding western <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Uttarakhand" title="Uttarakhand">Uttarakhand</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a></i>, literally meaning, "the language of the camp", a dialect of Hindustani, acquired official <a href="/wiki/Prestige_(sociolinguistics)" title="Prestige (sociolinguistics)">linguistic prestige</a> in the later <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughal period</a> (1800s). In the late 19th century, the movement standardising a written language from Khariboli, for the Indian masses in North India, started to standardise Hindi as a separate language from Urdu, which was learnt by the Mughal elite. In 1881, <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a> accepted Hindi as its sole official language, replacing Urdu, and thus became the first state of India to adopt Hindi.</p>
<p>After independence, the government of India instituted the following conventions:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (August 2011)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li>standardisation of grammar: In 1954, the Government of India set up a committee to prepare a grammar of Hindi; The committee's report was released in 1958 as <i>A Basic Grammar of Modern Hindi</i>.</li>
<li>standardisation of the orthography, using the <a href="/wiki/Devanagari" title="Devanagari">Devanagari</a> script, by the Central Hindi Directorate of the Ministry of Education and Culture to bring about uniformity in writing, to improve the shape of some Devanagari characters, and introducing diacritics to express sounds from other languages.</li>
</ul>
<p>The Constituent Assembly adopted Hindi as an <a href="/wiki/Languages_with_official_status_in_India" title="Languages with official status in India">official language of India</a> on 14 September 1949. Now, it is celebrated as <a href="/wiki/Hindi_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindi Day">Hindi Day</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_Modern_Standard_Urdu">Comparison with Modern Standard Urdu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Comparison with Modern Standard Urdu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hindi%E2%80%93Urdu_controversy" title="Hindi–Urdu controversy">Hindi–Urdu controversy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_phonology" title="Hindustani phonology">Hindustani phonology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_grammar" title="Hindustani grammar">Hindustani grammar</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">Linguistically</a>, Hindi and <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a> are two registers of the same language.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Hindi is written in the <a href="/wiki/Devanagari" title="Devanagari">Devanagari</a> script and uses more <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> words, whereas <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a> is written in the <a href="/wiki/Perso-Arabic" class="mw-redirect" title="Perso-Arabic">Perso-Arabic</a> script and uses more Arabic and Persian words. Hindi is the most commonly used official language in India. Urdu is the official language of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> along with English, and is one of the 22 official languages of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Script">Script</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Script">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Devanagari" title="Devanagari">Devanagari</a></div>
<p>Hindi is written in Devanagari script (देवनागरी लिपि <i>devanāgarī lipi</i>). Devanagari consists of 11 <a href="/wiki/Devanagari_script#Vowels" class="mw-redirect" title="Devanagari script">vowels</a> and 33 <a href="/wiki/Devanagari_script#consonants" class="mw-redirect" title="Devanagari script">consonants</a> and is written from left to right. It is an <a href="/wiki/Abugida" title="Abugida">abugida</a> as well.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romanization">Romanization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Romanization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Devanagari_transliteration" title="Devanagari transliteration">Devanagari transliteration</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_India" title="Government of India">Government of India</a> uses <a href="/wiki/Hunterian_transliteration" title="Hunterian transliteration">Hunterian transliteration</a> as its official system of writing Hindi in the Latin script. Various other systems also exist, such as <a href="/wiki/IAST" class="mw-redirect" title="IAST">IAST</a>, <a href="/wiki/ITRANS" title="ITRANS">ITRANS</a> and <a href="/wiki/ISO_15919" title="ISO 15919">ISO 15919</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">Vocabulary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_etymology" title="Hindustani etymology">Hindustani etymology</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Sanskrit_and_Persian_roots_in_Hindi" title="List of Sanskrit and Persian roots in Hindi">List of Sanskrit and Persian roots in Hindi</a></div>
<p>Traditionally, Hindi words are divided into five principal categories according to their etymology:</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Tatsama" title="Tatsama">Tatsama</a></b> (तत्सम "same as that") words: These are words which are spelled the same in Hindi as in Sanskrit (except for the absence of final case inflections).<sup id="cite_ref-sirysq_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sirysq-13">[13]</a></sup> They include words inherited from Sanskrit via <a href="/wiki/Prakrit" title="Prakrit">Prakrit</a> which have survived without modification (e.g. Hindi नाम <i>nām</i> / Sanskrit नाम <i>nāma</i>, "name"; Hindi कर्म <i>karm</i> / Sanskrit कर्म <i>karma</i>, "deed, action; <a href="/wiki/Karma" title="Karma">karma</a>"),<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> as well as forms borrowed directly from Sanskrit in more modern times (e.g. प्रार्थना <i>prārthanā</i>, "prayer").<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Pronunciation, however, conforms to Hindi norms and may differ from that of classical Sanskrit. Amongst nouns, the <i>tatsam</i> word could be the Sanskrit non-inflected word-stem, or it could be the nominative singular form in the Sanskrit nominal declension.</li>
<li><b>Ardhatatsama</b> (अर्धतत्सम "semi-tatsama") words: Such words have typically undergone sound changes subsequent to being borrowed. (e.g. Hindi सूरज <i>sūraj</i> from Sanskrit सूर्य <i>surya</i>)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Tadbhava" title="Tadbhava">Tadbhava</a></b> (तद्भव "born of that") words: These are words that are spelled differently from in Sanskrit but are derivable from a Sanskrit prototype by phonological rules (e.g. Sanskrit कर्म <i>karma</i>, "deed" becomes <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a> कम्म <i>kamma</i>, and eventually Hindi काम <i>kām</i>, "work").<sup id="cite_ref-sirysq_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sirysq-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Deshaj</b> (देशज) words: These are words that were not borrowings but do not derive from attested Indo-Aryan words either. Belonging to this category are <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Onomatopoetic">onomatopoetic</a> words or ones borrowed from local non-<a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">Indo-Aryan</a> languages.</li>
<li><b>Videshī</b> (विदेशी "foreign") words: These include all <a href="/wiki/Loanwords" class="mw-redirect" title="Loanwords">loanwords</a> purportedly from non-indigenous languages. The most frequent sources identified in this category have been <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>. Examples are कमेटी <i>kameṭī</i> from English <i>committee</i> and Hindi साबुन <i>sābun</i> "soap" from Arabic].</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sanskrit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Much of Modern Standard Hindi's vocabulary is derived from Sanskrit, either as <i>tatsama</i> or <i>tadbhava</i>, especially in technical and academic field. The Hindi standard, from which much of the Persian, Arabic and English vocabulary has been purged and replaced by <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologisms</a> compounding <i>tatsam</i> words, is called <i>Shuddha Hindi</i> (pure Hindi), and is viewed as a more prestigious dialect over other more colloquial forms of Hindi.</p>
<p>Excessive use of <i>tatsama</i> words creates problems for native speakers. They may have Sanskrit consonant clusters which do not exist in native Hindi. The educated middle class of India may be able to pronounce such words, but others have difficulty. Persian and Arabic vocabulary given 'authentic' pronunciations cause similar difficulty.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Persian">Persian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Persian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hindi also features significant <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> influence, standardised from spoken <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Hindustani</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kachru_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kachru-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2016)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Many have come to take the place of <i>tatsama</i> vocabulary, such as दरवाज़ा <i>darvāzā</i> "door" (<i>tatsama</i> द्वारा <i>dvārā</i>), and many more are used alongside <i>tatsama</i> words.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic">Arabic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Arabic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> also shows influence in Hindi, often via Persian but sometimes directly.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hindi_literature" title="Hindi literature">Hindi literature</a></div>
<p>Hindi literature is broadly divided into four prominent forms or styles, being <i><a href="/wiki/Bhakti" title="Bhakti">Bhakti</a></i> (devotional – <a href="/wiki/Kabirdas" class="mw-redirect" title="Kabirdas">Kabir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raskhan" title="Raskhan">Raskhan</a>); <i>Shringar</i> (beauty – <a href="/wiki/Keshav" class="mw-redirect" title="Keshav">Keshav</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bihari_(poet)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bihari (poet)">Bihari</a>); <i>Virgatha</i> (extolling brave warriors); and <i>Adhunik</i> (modern).</p>
<p>Medieval Hindi literature is marked by the influence of <a href="/wiki/Bhakti" title="Bhakti">Bhakti</a> movement and the composition of long, epic poems. It was primarily written in other <a href="/wiki/Hindi_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindi languages">varieties of Hindi</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Avadhi" class="mw-redirect" title="Avadhi">Avadhi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Braj_Bhasha" title="Braj Bhasha">Braj Bhasha</a>, but also in <a href="/wiki/Khariboli" class="mw-redirect" title="Khariboli">Khariboli</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British Raj</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Hindustani</a> became the prestige dialect. Hindustani with heavily <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskritised</a> vocabulary or <i>Sahityik</i> Hindi (Literary Hindi) was popularised by the writings of <a href="/wiki/Swami_Dayananda_Saraswati" class="mw-redirect" title="Swami Dayananda Saraswati">Swami Dayananda Saraswati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhartendu_Harishchandra" class="mw-redirect" title="Bhartendu Harishchandra">Bhartendu Harishchandra</a> and others. The rising numbers of newspapers and magazines made Hindustani popular with the educated people.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chandrakanta_(novel)" title="Chandrakanta (novel)">Chandrakanta</a>, written by <a href="/wiki/Devaki_Nandan_Khatri" title="Devaki Nandan Khatri">Devaki Nandan Khatri</a> in 1888, is considered the first authentic work of prose in modern Hindi.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The person who brought realism in the Hindi prose literature was <a href="/wiki/Munshi_Premchand" class="mw-redirect" title="Munshi Premchand">Munshi Premchand</a>, who is considered as the most revered figure in the world of Hindi fiction and progressive movement.</p>
<p>The <i>Dwivedi Yug</i> ("Age of Dwivedi") in Hindi literature lasted from 1900 to 1918. It is named after <a href="/wiki/Mahavir_Prasad_Dwivedi" title="Mahavir Prasad Dwivedi">Mahavir Prasad Dwivedi</a>, who played a major role in establishing the Modern Hindi language in poetry and broadening the acceptable subjects of Hindi poetry from the traditional ones of religion and romantic love.</p>
<p>In the 20th century, Hindi literature saw a romantic upsurge. This is known as <i><a href="/wiki/Chhayavaad" title="Chhayavaad">Chhayavaad</a></i> (<i>shadowism</i>) and the literary figures belonging to this school are known as <i>Chhayavaadi</i>. <a href="/wiki/Jaishankar_Prasad" title="Jaishankar Prasad">Jaishankar Prasad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suryakant_Tripathi_%27Nirala%27" title="Suryakant Tripathi 'Nirala'">Suryakant Tripathi 'Nirala'</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mahadevi_Varma" title="Mahadevi Varma">Mahadevi Varma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sumitranandan_Pant" title="Sumitranandan Pant">Sumitranandan Pant</a>, are the four major <i>Chhayavaadi</i> poets.</p>
<p><i>Uttar Adhunik</i> is the post-modernist period of Hindi literature, marked by a questioning of early trends that copied the West as well as the excessive ornamentation of the <a href="/wiki/Chhayavaad" title="Chhayavaad">Chhayavaadi</a> movement, and by a return to simple language and natural themes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sample_text">Sample text</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hindi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sample text">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">See also: <a href="/wiki/Urdu#Examples" title="Urdu">Urdu §&#160;Examples</a></div>
<p>The following is a sample text in High Hindi, of the Article 1 of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a> (by the United Nations):</p>
<dl>
<dt>Hindi</dt>
<dd>अनुच्छेद 1 (एक) <i>–</i> सभी मनुष्यों को गौरव और अधिकारों के विषय में जन्मजात स्वतन्त्रता और समानता प्राप्त हैं। उन्हें बुद्धि और अन्तरात्मा की देन प्राप्त है और परस्पर उन्हें भाईचारे के भाव से बर्ताव करना चाहिए।</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Transliteration (<a href="/wiki/International_Alphabet_of_Sanskrit_Transliteration" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">IAST</a>)</dt>
<dd><i>Anucched 1 (ek) – Sabhī manuṣyõ ko gaurav aur adhikārõ ke viṣay mẽ janmajāt svatantratā aur samāntā prāpt hai. Unhẽ buddhi aur antarātmā kī den prāpt hai aur paraspar unhẽ bhāīcāre ke bhāv se bartāv karnā cāhie.</i></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Transcription (<a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">IPA</a>)</dt>
<dd><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ənʊtʃʰːeːd̪ eːk | səbʱiː mənʊʃjõː koː ɡɔːɾəʋ ɔːr əd̪ʱɪkaːɾõ keː maːmleː mẽː dʒənmədʒaːt̪ sʋət̪ənt̪ɾət̪aː ɔːr səmaːntaː pɾaːpt̪ hɛː ‖ ʊnʱẽ bʊd̪ʱːɪ ɔːɾ ənt̪əɾaːt̪maː kiː d̪eːn pɾaːpt̪ hɛː ɔːɾ pəɾəspəɾ ʊnʱẽː bʱaːiːtʃaːɾeː keː bʱaːʋ seː bəɾt̪aːʋ kəɾnə tʃaːhɪeː ‖]</span></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Gloss (word-to-word)</dt>
<dd>Article 1 (one) <i>–</i> All human-beings to dignity and rights' matter in from-birth freedom and equality acquired is. Them to reason and conscience's endowment acquired is and always them to brotherhood's spirit with behaviour to do should.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Translation (grammatical)</dt>
<dd>Article 1 <i>–</i> All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights. They are endowed with reason and conscience and should act towards one another in a spirit of brotherhood.</dd>
</dl>
